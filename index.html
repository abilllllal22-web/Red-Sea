The Water Body

One Sea.
Eight Nations.

The Red Sea is a semi-enclosed inlet of the Indian Ocean stretching 2,250 km between Africa and the Arabian Peninsula. It is one of the most strategically critical bodies of water on Earth — and one of the most threatened. Eight nations share its shores, its fish, its trade routes, and its fate. Wikipedia 2024

Its 2,000 km of fringing coral reefs — 5,000 to 7,000 years old — are among the most biodiverse and heat-resistant on the planet. Its coral ecosystems may be humanity's last refuge as oceans warm. UNESCO 2024

At the same time, this fragile system carries 12–15% of all global maritime trade through the Suez Canal — connecting Europe to Asia, supporting millions of jobs, and keeping the price of goods stable worldwide. Wikipedia 2024

"The Red Sea is bordered by eight countries, some of which have no official ties with each other or are in active conflict — yet all share the same ecosystem." Kleinhaus et al., Frontiers in Marine Science, 2020

The Crisis

A Sea Under
Siege

Emergency Alert: Ongoing Environmental Destruction
Since November 2023, over 190 Houthi attacks on commercial shipping have triggered oil spills, ecological collapse, and a 57.5% collapse in Suez Canal transit volumes. The damage is not distributed equally — the poorest nations suffer most. Atlas Institute 2025
November 2023
The Campaign Begins
Yemen's Houthi movement launches its first attacks on Red Sea shipping, framing it as solidarity with Gaza. Within weeks, major shipping companies begin rerouting via the Cape of Good Hope — adding 10–14 days to every Asia-Europe voyage. Atlas Institute 2025
February 2024
MV Rubymar — The First Environmental Catastrophe
A Houthi missile strikes the bulk carrier MV Rubymar, creating an 18-mile oil slick. More alarming: the ship was carrying 21,000 metric tons of ammonium-based fertilizer — enough to trigger massive algal blooms devastating coral reefs and fishing grounds across Yemen's coast. AGSI 2025
August 2024
MT Sounion — Closest to Catastrophe
The Greek-flagged oil tanker Sounion is attacked and set ablaze near Hodeidah. Carrying enough crude oil to trigger the largest oil spill of the 21st century, an EU-led salvage mission (Operation Aspides) narrowly averts environmental disaster. PAX 2025
July 2025
Eritrea's Coastline at Risk
Two more ships are sunk in six days. Oil slicks drift within 13 km of Eritrea's Barasole Marine Reserve — a 150,000-hectare biodiversity hotspot for sea turtles, mangroves, and unique coral. Eritrea has no capacity to deploy emergency clean-up. PAX 2025
2023–2025
The Human Cost: Yemen's Fishers
Of Yemen's 300,000 coastal fishers — already surviving a catastrophic civil war — over 60% lost their livelihoods. The ecological destruction of their fishing grounds, combined with famine, created a humanitarian emergency without precedent. Mongabay 2024
"The Houthis' attacks raised serious environmental concerns regarding marine ecosystems and wildlife, as the Red Sea supports coral ecosystems and rare aquatic species, including mangroves that protect the coastline." Frontiers in Political Science, 2025
The Ecology

A World Beneath
the Surface

The Red Sea is home to extraordinary biodiversity — and its corals are uniquely positioned as a global refuge from climate change. What makes it irreplaceable, and what stands to be lost forever? EBSCO 2024

🐠
Marine Fish Species
The Red Sea hosts an extraordinary array of fish species, with around 14.7% found nowhere else on Earth — some of the highest endemism rates of any sea on the planet.
1,200+
🪸
Coral Species
Over 200 coral species form 2,000 km of fringing reefs, 5,000–7,000 years old. Crucially, these corals withstand temperatures that bleach reefs elsewhere — making the Red Sea a potential climate refuge. UNESCO 2024
200+
🦑
Invertebrate Species
From nudibranch to dugong, the Red Sea supports over 1,000 invertebrate species on its shallow shelves, plus five species of sea turtle and vast seagrass meadows vital for carbon sequestration. EBSCO 2024
1,000+
🏛️
No Reef-Wide Management
Despite being split between 8 nations, there are currently no coordinated scientific research or management efforts covering the entire Red Sea reef complex — unlike the Great Barrier Reef or Coral Triangle. Kleinhaus 2020
🌡️
Climate Refuge Under Threat
The Gulf of Aqaba's corals could be one of the planet's largest marine refuges from climate change — but only if anthropogenic stressors are immediately reduced. Oil spills, overfishing, and pollution imperil this global treasure. UNESCO 2024
🎣
Fisheries in Decline
Nearly all Egyptian artisanal fishers report declining catches. 61% blame overfishing. Modern commercial fleets harvest immature fish unable to reproduce. Egyptian Red Sea fishers earn just 54% of average rural income. Pinnegar 2024
The Bordering States

Eight Nations,
One Fate

The Red Sea is shared by eight countries spanning two continents — with vastly different economies, political systems, and levels of environmental capacity. This inequality is at the heart of the justice crisis. Wikipedia 2024

🇪🇬
Egypt
Operates the Suez Canal. Lost billions in revenue from the shipping crisis. Home to world-famous dive resorts: Hurghada, Dahab, Sharm el-Sheikh.
🇸🇦
Saudi Arabia
Hosts PERSGA Secretariat. Largest economy on the Red Sea. Investing in NEOM and coral reef protection via Saudi Green Initiative.
🇾🇪
Yemen
In active civil war. 300,000+ coastal fishers, 60%+ unemployed. Source of the Houthi crisis. Bears both the political and ecological burden.
🇸🇩
Sudan
Home to Sanganeb Marine National Park — a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Limited maritime enforcement capacity. Relies on Red Sea fisheries for food security.
🇪🇷
Eritrea
Coastline threatened by oil slicks in 2025. Home to the Dahlak Archipelago with 350+ islands. Minimal capacity to respond to environmental emergencies.
🇩🇯
Djibouti
Strategic gateway at the Bab el-Mandeb Strait. Hosts major international military bases. A key node in global shipping routes.
🇯🇴
Jordan
Controls only a narrow strip at the Gulf of Aqaba. Aqaba is a major Red Sea dive tourism hub. Involved in Red Sea–Dead Sea water conveyance discussions.
🇮🇱
Israel
Borders the Gulf of Aqaba at Eilat. No formal diplomatic ties with most Red Sea neighbors. Coral reefs here are studied as climate-change refuge candidates.
Critical Analysis

The SEEP Framework

To understand the Red Sea crisis in full, we analyse it through four interconnected lenses: Social, Economic, Environmental, and Political. Each dimension reveals a different dimension of the fairness and justice crisis.

S
Social Perspective
Who Bears the Cost?
  • 300,000 Yemeni coastal fishers, with 60%+ losing livelihoods due to the ecological disruption of their fishing grounds. Mongabay 2024
  • Egyptian artisanal fishers earn just 54% of average rural income — extreme poverty before ecological crises begin. Pinnegar 2024
  • Eritrean and Djiboutian coastal communities face pollution with no capacity to deploy clean-up operations.
  • Implication: The most vulnerable populations bear the greatest costs while lacking political voice in management decisions — a profound fairness failure.
E
Economic Perspective
Who Captures the Wealth?
  • Suez Canal transit volumes dropped 57.5%, costing Egypt billions in revenues. Atlas Institute 2025
  • Red Sea tourism generates ~$12 billion/year — but Yemen and Eritrea receive negligible shares due to conflict and infrastructure gaps. Red Sea Creatures 2025
  • Fisheries produce $230 million/year but benefits are distributed unequally between nations.
  • Implication: Economic benefits concentrate in wealthier states while ecological costs fall on poorer ones — violating the principle of fair development.
E
Environmental Perspective
What Is Being Lost?
  • The MV Rubymar's 21,000 tonnes of fertilizer threatens algal blooms and oxygen-dead zones in coral habitat. AGSI 2025
  • July 2025 oil slicks drifted within 13 km of Eritrea's Barasole Reserve — a 150,000-hectare biodiversity hotspot. PAX 2025
  • No reef-wide management system exists despite the global uniqueness and climate-refuge potential of Red Sea corals. Kleinhaus 2020
  • Implication: If these corals are destroyed, humanity loses one of its last potential refuges as oceans warm — an irreversible global loss.
P
Political Perspective
Why Can't We Fix It?
  • The Red Sea borders eight nations, some in active conflict, some with no diplomatic relations — preventing coordinated management. Kleinhaus 2020
  • PERSGA exists since 1974 but lacks enforcement power; cannot compel compliance from a country in civil war. UNEP 2024
  • Military responses (Operation Prosperity Guardian, Operation Aspides) address security but not root ecological causes.
  • Implication: Political fragmentation is the root obstacle to fair management. Resolution requires diplomacy alongside ecology.
Expert Recommendation

Three Interventions
for a Just Future

As experts of the area, we propose three interconnected, evidence-based interventions that address the social, economic, environmental, and political dimensions of the Red Sea crisis simultaneously.

I
Red Sea Emergency Environmental Fund
All eight bordering nations — contributing proportionally to their GDP — establish a PERSGA-administered rapid-response fund for oil spill clean-up and ecological restoration. No coastline should be left unprotected simply because its government lacks resources. Contributions from wealthier states (Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Israel) subsidize the capacity of poorer ones (Yemen, Eritrea, Djibouti). This directly addresses the social and economic justice gap by ensuring that a pollution event in Eritrean waters receives the same response as one in Saudi waters. UNEP 2024
II
Red Sea Marine Refuge Protocol
A binding multilateral treaty designating the Gulf of Aqaba and northern Red Sea corals as a UNESCO-backed Marine Refuge Zone — with a reef-wide monitoring network, standardized data sharing across all eight nations, and a moratorium on destructive fishing practices within protected zones. Modelled on the Great Barrier Reef Marine Park and Coral Triangle Initiative, this addresses the critical gap identified by Kleinhaus et al. (2020): there is currently no coordinated reef-wide scientific management despite the global importance of these corals. Kleinhaus 2020
III
Equitable Revenue Sharing Mechanism
A percentage of Suez Canal revenues and Red Sea tourism income to be redistributed through an international development fund — managed by PERSGA with UN oversight — to Yemen, Eritrea, Djibouti, and Sudan. This fund would finance sustainable fisheries management programmes, marine protected area enforcement, and artisanal fisher support schemes. Economic justice is inseparable from ecological sustainability: communities living in poverty cannot be expected to protect marine ecosystems they depend on for survival. Red Sea Creatures 2025
Your Role

The Sea Needs
Your Voice

The Red Sea does not belong to governments or shipping companies. It belongs to the 28 million people who live on its shores, the billions who depend on its trade routes, and the generations who will inherit whatever we leave behind.

01
Demand PERSGA Commitment
Pressure your government to strengthen its obligations under the Jeddah Convention and contribute to the proposed Emergency Environmental Fund. Environmental diplomacy must match economic diplomacy. UNEP 2024
02
Choose Sustainable Seafood
Reject fish caught through destructive trawl fishing. Support Egypt's 2024 moratorium and advocate for similar protections across all Red Sea nations. Your purchasing decisions directly affect fish stock recovery. HEPCA 2024
03
Advocate for Peace
The environmental destruction will not end without resolving the Yemeni civil war. Political solutions — not just naval patrols — are required. Ending the conflict is an ecological imperative, not just a humanitarian one. Frontiers 2025
04
Support Red Sea NGOs
Organizations like HEPCA, PERSGA, and PAX for Peace work on the frontlines of Red Sea protection. Support their research, advocacy, and community programs that give voice to the fishers and coastal communities most affected.
05
Educate Others
Share this information. Most citizens are unaware that oil slicks from Houthi attacks are threatening the world's most climate-resistant coral reefs. Awareness is the first step toward political will for change.
06
Report Violations
If you witness illegal fishing, waste dumping, or coral damage in Red Sea waters, report it to national environmental authorities and international bodies like PERSGA. Enforcement depends on citizen vigilance. PERSGA 2025
